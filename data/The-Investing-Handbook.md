## 1. Why Invest at All

*Investing is the act of putting money to work today in the expectation of more money — or more purchasing power — in the future.*

Saving and investing are related but different. **Saving** means setting money aside, usually in cash or a near-cash account, where it is safe and instantly available. **Investing** means committing money to assets that can grow in value or produce income, accepting some risk of loss in exchange for the chance of a higher return. You need both: savings for safety and short-term needs, investments for long-term growth.

### The two forces that make investing necessary

**Inflation** quietly erodes the value of cash. If prices rise around 3% per year, money sitting under a mattress loses roughly a third of its purchasing power over a decade. Doing nothing is not neutral — it is a slow, guaranteed loss in real terms.

**Compounding** works in the opposite direction and is the single most important idea in all of finance. When your returns themselves earn returns, growth accelerates over time. The longer the runway, the more dramatic the effect — which is why *time in the market* matters more than almost anything else.

> **Example — the power of compounding.** Invest $10,000 and add $500 a month at a 7% average annual return. After 10 years you'd have roughly $106,000; after 20 years about $284,000; after 30 years about $640,000. You contributed $190,000 of that over 30 years — the remaining ~$450,000 is growth on growth. Starting ten years earlier can easily double your final result.

### The Rule of 72

A quick mental shortcut: divide 72 by your annual return rate to estimate how many years it takes money to double. At 7%, money doubles roughly every 10 years. At 10%, about every 7 years. It illustrates why small differences in return — and in fees — compound into very large differences over a lifetime.

> **Key idea.** The three levers you control are **how much you invest**, **how long you stay invested**, and **how much you pay in fees and taxes**. You cannot control the market's returns. Most successful investing is about getting the controllable levers right and then being patient.

## 2. The Core Principles

*A handful of durable principles underlie almost every sound investment decision.*

**1. Return is compensation for risk.** In efficient markets, you are generally not paid more without taking on more risk. Assets that promise high returns with little risk are either misunderstood or too good to be true. Expected return and risk move together.

**2. Time horizon changes everything.** Money you need next year should not be in volatile assets; money you won't touch for 20 years generally should be. A long horizon lets you ride out downturns and harvest the higher long-run returns of stocks. A short horizon demands stability.

**3. Diversification is the only "free lunch."** Spreading money across many investments reduces the impact of any single one failing, without necessarily reducing expected return. It is the rare case in finance where you lower risk without giving up much reward.

**4. Costs are certain; returns are not.** You cannot guarantee a 7% return, but you can guarantee that a 1% annual fee will cost you 1% every year, compounding against you. Minimizing controllable costs is one of the most reliable ways to improve outcomes.

**5. Markets are hard to beat consistently.** Decades of evidence show that the large majority of professional, full-time fund managers fail to outperform a simple low-cost index over long periods, after fees. This is the central argument for passive, index-based investing for most people.

**6. Behavior beats brilliance.** The biggest destroyer of returns is not picking the "wrong" investment — it is panic-selling in downturns, chasing fads, and abandoning a plan. A mediocre strategy followed consistently usually beats a brilliant one abandoned at the first scare.

> **Foundations first.** Before investing seriously, most guidance suggests three prerequisites: (1) high-interest debt (like credit cards) paid down, (2) an emergency fund of roughly 3–6 months of expenses in cash, and (3) any employer retirement match captured in full. Investing while carrying 20%+ credit-card interest is usually a losing trade.

## 3. Risk, Return & Diversification

*Understanding risk is what separates investing from gambling.*

### What "risk" actually means

Risk is not a single thing. The main types every investor should recognize:

| Type of risk | What it is |
|---|---|
| Market risk | The whole market can fall; broad declines hit nearly everything. |
| Inflation risk | Your returns may not keep pace with rising prices, eroding real value. |
| Interest-rate risk | Rising rates push down the price of existing bonds and rate-sensitive assets. |
| Credit / default risk | A bond issuer or borrower may fail to pay you back. |
| Liquidity risk | You may not be able to sell quickly without accepting a lower price. |
| Concentration risk | Too much in one stock, sector, or asset magnifies a single point of failure. |
| Longevity / shortfall risk | Being too conservative, so your money runs out or fails to grow enough. |

### Volatility vs. permanent loss

A crucial distinction: **volatility** is temporary price movement up and down; **permanent loss** is destroyed capital you never recover. A diversified index that drops 30% in a recession is volatile but historically recovers. A single company that goes bankrupt is a permanent loss. Diversification converts much potential permanent loss into mere volatility you can wait out.

### How diversification works

When you own many assets that don't all move together, their individual ups and downs partly cancel out, smoothing the ride. The key is **low correlation** — owning things that respond differently to the same events. Stocks and high-quality bonds, for instance, have often moved in opposite directions during crises.

> **Diversification in practice.** Owning 30 stocks in one country's tech sector is far less diversified than owning a single global index fund holding thousands of companies across every sector and region. True diversification spans *companies, sectors, geographies, and asset classes* — not just the number of holdings.

### Measuring risk: a few common metrics

- **Standard deviation** — how much returns bounce around their average; a common proxy for volatility.
- **Beta** — how much an asset moves relative to the overall market (beta of 1 moves with the market; above 1 is more volatile).
- **Maximum drawdown** — the largest peak-to-trough fall; tests whether you could stomach the worst case.
- **Sharpe ratio** — return earned per unit of risk; higher is better.

> **Risk tolerance vs. risk capacity.** **Tolerance** is how much volatility you can emotionally handle; **capacity** is how much you can financially afford to lose given your goals and timeline. A good portfolio respects the lower of the two. Knowing you'll panic-sell is itself a reason to hold less in stocks.

## 4. The Major Asset Classes

*Every portfolio is built from a small set of fundamental building blocks, each with a distinct risk-and-return profile.*

| Asset class | What it is | Typical role | Main risks |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Stocks (equities)** | Ownership shares in companies | Long-term growth engine | High volatility, business failure |
| **Bonds (fixed income)** | Loans to governments or companies that pay interest | Income, stability, ballast | Rates, inflation, default |
| **Cash & equivalents** | Savings, money-market funds, T-bills, CDs | Safety, liquidity, dry powder | Inflation erodes value |
| **Real estate** | Property directly or via REITs | Income + inflation hedge | Illiquidity, leverage, local markets |
| **Commodities** | Gold, oil, agriculture, etc. | Diversifier, inflation hedge | No income, very volatile |
| **Alternatives** | Private equity, hedge funds, collectibles | Diversification, niche returns | Illiquid, opaque, high fees |
| **Crypto / digital** | Bitcoin and other digital assets | Speculative growth (small slice) | Extreme volatility, regulatory |

### Stocks

When you buy a stock, you own a fractional piece of a real business. You profit two ways: **capital appreciation** (the price rises) and **dividends** (a share of profits paid out). Over long stretches of history, broad stock markets have produced the highest returns of the major asset classes — roughly 7% per year after inflation in long-run U.S. data — but with deep, frightening drops along the way. Stocks are the growth engine of most portfolios.

### Bonds

A bond is an IOU: you lend money and receive periodic interest plus your principal back at maturity. Bonds are generally steadier than stocks and often rise when stocks fall, which is why they act as portfolio ballast. Key features are the **coupon** (interest rate), **maturity** (when principal is repaid), and **credit quality** (likelihood of repayment). Prices move opposite to interest rates: when rates rise, existing bonds become less attractive and their prices fall.

### Cash and equivalents

This is your safety and liquidity layer: high-yield savings, money-market funds, Treasury bills, and certificates of deposit (CDs). It won't grow your wealth much, and inflation slowly eats it, but it's there when you need it and lets you avoid selling investments at a bad time.

### Real estate

You can own property directly (rental income plus appreciation, but with effort, leverage, and illiquidity) or indirectly through **REITs** — Real Estate Investment Trusts that trade like stocks and pass most of their income to shareholders. REITs offer real-estate exposure without the hassle of being a landlord.

### Commodities, alternatives, and crypto

These are diversifiers and, for most people, a small optional slice rather than a core holding. Commodities like gold can hedge inflation but produce no income. Alternatives can diversify but are often illiquid and expensive. Crypto is highly speculative and extremely volatile — only ever an amount you can afford to lose entirely.

> **A useful mental model.** Stocks are for *growth*, bonds are for *stability and income*, cash is for *safety and flexibility*, and everything else is a *diversifier*. Most of your long-term return will come from how you split money among these — far more than from picking individual winners within them.

## 5. Investment Vehicles: Funds, ETFs & More

*You rarely buy asset classes directly — you buy them through "vehicles" that package investments together.*

### Individual securities

Buying single stocks or bonds gives maximum control but maximum concentration risk and requires real research. For most people it's a small, optional part of a portfolio — not the foundation.

### Mutual funds

A mutual fund pools money from many investors to buy a diversified basket of securities, managed professionally. They trade once per day at the closing price. They can be **actively managed** (a manager tries to beat the market, charging higher fees) or **passive / index** (simply tracking a market index at very low cost).

### Index funds

An index fund passively tracks a market benchmark — for example, the S&P 500 (large U.S. companies) or a total-world index. Because there's no expensive manager trying to pick winners, fees are minimal, and decades of data show this approach beats most active funds over time. For the typical investor, low-cost broad index funds are the workhorse.

### ETFs (Exchange-Traded Funds)

ETFs are like index funds but trade on an exchange throughout the day like a stock. They're usually low-cost, tax-efficient, and require no minimum beyond one share's price. Most broad-market ETFs and index mutual funds are functionally similar; the differences are mechanical (intraday trading, tax handling, minimums).

| Vehicle | Diversification | Typical cost | Trades | Best for |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Individual stock | None on its own | Commission (often $0) | Intraday | Conviction picks, small slice |
| Active mutual fund | High | 0.5%–1.5%+/yr | End of day | Specific active strategies |
| Index mutual fund | High | ~0.02%–0.20%/yr | End of day | Core long-term holdings |
| ETF (broad index) | High | ~0.03%–0.20%/yr | Intraday | Flexible core holdings |
| Target-date fund | Very high | ~0.10%–0.65%/yr | End of day | Hands-off retirement saving |

### Target-date funds

A target-date fund (e.g., "Target 2055") is an all-in-one portfolio that automatically becomes more conservative as the target year approaches. It's the simplest possible hands-off option — one fund holding a globally diversified mix that rebalances itself. Excellent for people who want to "set it and forget it," especially inside a retirement account.

> **The simplest sensible portfolio.** Many experts argue a beginner can do extremely well with just **one** low-cost target-date fund, or a "three-fund portfolio" of a total U.S. stock index, a total international stock index, and a total bond index. Complexity is optional and often counterproductive.

## 6. Accounts & Tax-Advantaged Wrappers

*Where you hold investments can matter as much as what you hold. The "account" is a wrapper with its own tax rules.* **(U.S. figures, 2026)**

Think of accounts in two buckets: **taxable brokerage accounts** (flexible, no contribution limits, but you owe tax on gains and income) and **tax-advantaged accounts** (powerful tax breaks in exchange for rules and limits). A common priority order is: capture the full employer match, then max tax-advantaged space, then invest the rest in a taxable account.

### The key U.S. tax-advantaged accounts

| Account | Tax treatment | 2026 contribution limit | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **401(k) / 403(b)** (Traditional) | Pre-tax in, taxed on withdrawal | $24,500 (+$8,000 catch-up at 50+; $11,250 at 60–63) | Employer plan; often includes a match |
| **Roth 401(k)** | After-tax in, tax-free out | Same $24,500 limit, shared with Traditional 401(k) | Good if you expect higher future tax rates |
| **Traditional IRA** | Possibly deductible in, taxed out | $7,500 (+$1,100 catch-up at 50+) | Deduction phases out at higher incomes if covered by a work plan |
| **Roth IRA** | After-tax in, tax-free out | $7,500, shared with Traditional IRA (+$1,100 at 50+) | Income limits apply; tax-free growth & withdrawals |
| **HSA** | Triple tax-advantaged | $4,400 self / $8,750 family (+$1,000 at 55+) | Requires an HSA-eligible high-deductible health plan |
| **529 plan** | Tax-free for education | Set by state (high; gift-tax rules apply) | For qualified education expenses |

*Source: IRS 2026 limit announcements (Notice 2025-67 and Rev. Proc. 2025-19). Roth IRA eligibility and Traditional IRA deductibility phase out over income ranges; check current IRS figures for your filing status.*

### Traditional vs. Roth — the core trade-off

**Traditional** accounts give you a tax break *today* (contributions reduce taxable income) and tax the money when you withdraw it in retirement. **Roth** accounts give no break today but grow and come out completely *tax-free* later. The simple rule of thumb: choose Traditional if you expect to be in a *lower* tax bracket in retirement, and Roth if you expect a *higher* one — and many people split between the two to hedge.

> **The HSA's "triple" advantage.** A Health Savings Account is uniquely powerful: contributions are tax-deductible, growth is tax-free, and withdrawals for qualified medical expenses are tax-free. After age 65 it can also be used like a Traditional IRA for any purpose. Many savers invest their HSA and pay medical costs out of pocket, letting the account compound for decades. (Note: a few states, including New Jersey and California, tax HSA contributions at the state level.)

> **Don't leave the match on the table.** An employer 401(k) match is an immediate, guaranteed return — often 50% to 100% on the matched portion. Contributing at least enough to capture the full match is almost always the highest-priority investing move you can make, ahead of nearly everything else.

## 7. Building & Managing a Portfolio

*A portfolio is the deliberate combination of assets designed to meet your goals at a risk level you can live with.*

### Step 1 — Define goals and time horizons

Different money has different jobs. A house down payment in 3 years, a child's college in 12 years, and retirement in 30 years call for very different allocations. Match the risk of each pool of money to when you'll need it.

### Step 2 — Set your asset allocation

**Asset allocation** — the split between stocks, bonds, and cash — is the single biggest driver of a portfolio's risk and return, far more than individual security selection. A classic illustrative starting point is to hold a stock percentage near "110 minus your age," though modern guidance often favors more stocks for those with long horizons and steady income.

| Sample profile | Stocks | Bonds | Character |
|---|---|---|---|
| Aggressive (long horizon) | 90% | 10% | Max growth, big swings |
| Growth | 80% | 20% | Growth-tilted, still volatile |
| Balanced | 60% | 40% | Classic moderate mix |
| Conservative | 40% | 60% | Stability-focused |
| Capital preservation | 20% | 80% | Near-term needs |

*Illustrative only — not recommendations. Your right allocation depends on your goals, horizon, and risk tolerance.*

### Step 3 — Diversify within each class

Inside stocks, spread across U.S. and international, large and small companies, and all sectors — most easily done with broad index funds. Inside bonds, mix maturities and high-quality issuers. The goal is no single failure can sink the whole ship.

### Step 4 — Rebalance periodically

Over time, winners grow and drift your allocation away from target (e.g., a 60/40 mix becomes 70/30 after a stock rally). **Rebalancing** — selling a bit of what's grown and buying what's lagged to restore your targets — enforces "buy low, sell high" mechanically and keeps risk in check. Once or twice a year, or when an allocation drifts more than ~5 percentage points, is plenty.

> **Asset location vs. asset allocation.** Beyond *what* you own, *where* you hold it matters. Tax-inefficient assets (like bonds and REITs) generally belong in tax-advantaged accounts, while tax-efficient broad stock index funds work well in taxable accounts. This "asset location" can quietly add to after-tax returns.

## 8. Investing Strategies & Styles

*There are many named approaches. Most successful long-term investors use a small number of simple, repeatable ones.*

### Passive vs. active

**Passive investing** aims to match the market cheaply via index funds, accepting market returns. **Active investing** tries to beat the market through selection and timing, at higher cost and with a poor long-run track record for most. The evidence strongly favors a passive core for the typical investor.

### Dollar-cost averaging (DCA)

Investing a fixed amount on a regular schedule regardless of price. You automatically buy more shares when prices are low and fewer when high, removing emotion and the impossible task of timing the market. Automating contributions is, for most people, the most reliable wealth-building habit there is.

### Buy-and-hold

Holding quality investments through ups and downs rather than trading in and out. It minimizes costs, taxes, and behavioral mistakes, and lets compounding run. "Time in the market beats timing the market" is the distilled wisdom.

### Value vs. growth

**Value** investing seeks companies trading cheaply relative to fundamentals (think Warren Buffett's lineage). **Growth** investing targets fast-expanding companies whose future earnings justify high prices today. Both have had long periods of out- and under-performance; a broad index naturally holds both.

### Income investing

Building a portfolio for steady cash flow — dividend-paying stocks, bonds, REITs — often favored by retirees who want their portfolio to pay them without selling holdings.

> **Strategies to treat with caution.** **Market timing** (jumping in and out to dodge crashes) reliably underperforms staying invested, because missing just a handful of the market's best days devastates returns. **Stock-picking** for the bulk of your money, **day trading**, and **leverage / margin** all dramatically raise risk and are where most beginners lose money. Speculation isn't investing.

> **The boring strategy that usually wins.** Automatically invest a fixed amount each month into low-cost, broadly diversified index funds inside tax-advantaged accounts; rebalance occasionally; and don't touch it for decades. It's unglamorous, requires no skill at prediction, and outperforms most sophisticated approaches after costs.

## 9. How to Analyze an Investment

*If you do venture beyond index funds, these are the tools used to size up an individual investment.*

### Fundamental analysis

Studying a company's actual business — its earnings, debt, growth, competitive position, and management — to estimate what it's truly worth, then comparing that to its price. Core sources are the income statement, balance sheet, and cash-flow statement.

**Common valuation metrics**

| Metric | What it tells you |
|---|---|
| **P/E ratio** (Price ÷ Earnings) | How much you pay per dollar of profit; a rough gauge of how "expensive" a stock is. |
| **EPS** (Earnings per share) | Company profit divided by shares; the engine behind the P/E. |
| **P/B ratio** (Price ÷ Book) | Price relative to net asset value; popular in value investing. |
| **Dividend yield** | Annual dividend as a % of price; income return. |
| **Debt-to-equity** | How leveraged (and therefore risky) the company is. |
| **Free cash flow** | Cash left after running and maintaining the business; hard to fake. |
| **ROE** (Return on equity) | How efficiently the company turns shareholder money into profit. |

### Technical analysis

Studying price charts and trading patterns to predict short-term movements, rather than business value. It's widely used by traders and widely debated; it has little bearing on long-term, buy-and-hold investing and is not necessary for index investors.

### Top-down vs. bottom-up

**Top-down** starts with the big picture (economy, interest rates, sectors) then narrows to specific investments. **Bottom-up** starts with individual companies on their own merits regardless of the macro backdrop. Many analysts blend both.

> **A reality check on analysis.** Markets already reflect publicly available information, so consistently finding mispriced bargains is extremely hard — it's why most professionals can't beat the index. Treat individual analysis as a small, deliberate part of your portfolio (often called "play money"), not the foundation of your financial future.

## 10. Costs, Fees & Taxes

*Fees and taxes are the silent, certain drag on returns — and among the few things you can actually control.*

### Investment fees

- **Expense ratio** — the annual % a fund charges. The difference between 0.05% and 1.00% sounds tiny but compounds enormously over decades.
- **Loads** — sales commissions on some mutual funds (front-end or back-end). Avoidable; favor no-load funds.
- **Trading commissions** — now often $0 for U.S. stocks and ETFs at major brokers.
- **Advisory fees** — a financial advisor charging ~1% of assets per year; robo-advisors charge far less (~0.25%).
- **Bid-ask spreads & expense drag** — small hidden costs from trading frequently.

> **Why 1% matters so much.** On a $500,000 portfolio growing at 7% over 30 years, a 1% annual fee instead of 0.05% can cost well over $300,000 in lost final value. Fees compound against you exactly the way returns compound for you. Minimizing them is one of the highest-certainty improvements available.

### Taxes on investments (U.S.)

- **Capital gains** — profit when you sell. *Long-term* gains (held over a year) are taxed at lower rates than *short-term* gains (taxed as ordinary income). Holding longer is tax-favored.
- **Dividends** — "qualified" dividends get the lower long-term rate; ordinary dividends don't.
- **Interest** — bond and savings interest is generally taxed as ordinary income.
- **Tax-loss harvesting** — selling losers to offset gains and reduce your tax bill.
- **Tax-advantaged accounts** — shelter growth from these taxes entirely (see Chapter 6).

> **Practical takeaway.** Favor low-expense-ratio index funds, avoid loads and unnecessary trading, hold investments long-term, and use tax-advantaged accounts first. These habits cost nothing and reliably lift your after-fee, after-tax returns — the only returns that actually reach your pocket.

## 11. Behavioral Finance & Common Mistakes

*The biggest risk to your returns is usually the person in the mirror. Knowing the traps is half the defense.*

### Cognitive biases that cost money

| Bias | How it sabotages you |
|---|---|
| Loss aversion | Losses hurt about twice as much as gains feel good, driving panic-selling at the bottom. |
| Recency bias | Assuming recent trends continue — buying after run-ups, fleeing after crashes. |
| Herd behavior | Chasing whatever's popular (bubbles, hot stocks) and selling in stampedes. |
| Overconfidence | Believing you can beat the market or time it; leads to overtrading. |
| Confirmation bias | Seeking only information that supports what you already believe. |
| Anchoring | Fixating on an irrelevant reference point, like the price you paid. |
| FOMO | Fear of missing out, driving speculative bets near peaks. |

### The classic, recurring mistakes

1. **Panic-selling in downturns**, locking in losses and missing the recovery.
2. **Trying to time the market** instead of staying invested.
3. **Chasing performance** — piling into last year's hottest fund or stock.
4. **Under-diversifying** — too much in one stock (often an employer's).
5. **Paying high fees** for active management that underperforms.
6. **Not starting early**, forfeiting compounding's most valuable years.
7. **Ignoring inflation** by holding too much cash long-term.
8. **Investing money needed soon** in volatile assets.
9. **Tinkering constantly** instead of leaving a sound plan alone.

> **The single best defense: automate and ignore.** Set up automatic monthly contributions into a diversified, low-cost plan, then deliberately check it rarely. Removing yourself from the moment-to-moment decisions sidesteps nearly every behavioral trap at once. The investors who do best are often those who simply forget the account exists.

## 12. A Step-by-Step Way to Start

*A concrete, ordered checklist that synthesizes everything above into action.*

1. **Stabilize the foundation.** Pay off high-interest debt and build an emergency fund of roughly 3–6 months of expenses in a high-yield savings account.
2. **Capture the full employer match.** If you have a 401(k) with a match, contribute at least enough to get all of it — it's free, guaranteed return.
3. **Clarify your goals and timelines.** Separate short-term needs from long-term goals; match risk to horizon.
4. **Choose your accounts.** Generally: match → then a Roth or Traditional IRA / HSA → then more 401(k) → then a taxable brokerage for the rest.
5. **Open an account.** A reputable low-cost brokerage with no account fees and access to broad index funds and ETFs.
6. **Pick a simple allocation.** One target-date fund, or a three-fund portfolio (U.S. stocks, international stocks, bonds) matched to your risk tolerance.
7. **Automate contributions.** Set a fixed monthly transfer and investment so it happens without willpower (dollar-cost averaging).
8. **Rebalance occasionally.** Once or twice a year, nudge back to your target mix.
9. **Increase contributions over time.** Raise the amount as your income grows; aim to invest a meaningful share of each raise.
10. **Leave it alone.** Avoid reacting to headlines and market swings. Stay the course for decades and let compounding work.

> **What "good enough" looks like.** A new investor who pays off credit cards, gets the match, automatically funds a Roth IRA into a single low-cost target-date fund every month, and ignores the news for 30 years will likely outperform the large majority of more "sophisticated" investors. Simplicity, consistency, and low costs are the whole game.

### A few trusted ways to keep learning

Look to independent, low-commercial sources: classic books such as *The Little Book of Common Sense Investing*, *A Random Walk Down Wall Street*, and *The Psychology of Money*; reputable nonprofit and government resources like Investor.gov (U.S. SEC) and the FINRA investor education site; and community references such as the Bogleheads wiki. Be wary of anyone selling guaranteed returns, "hot tips," or get-rich-quick systems.

## 13. Glossary of Key Terms

*Quick definitions for the vocabulary used throughout this handbook.*

- **Asset allocation** — The split of a portfolio among stocks, bonds, cash, and other classes.
- **Bear / Bull market** — A sustained market decline (bear, often −20%+) or rise (bull).
- **Bond** — A loan to a government or company that pays interest and returns principal.
- **Capital gain** — Profit from selling an asset for more than you paid.
- **Compounding** — Earning returns on your prior returns, accelerating growth over time.
- **Correlation** — The degree to which two assets move together; low correlation aids diversification.
- **Diversification** — Spreading money across many investments to reduce risk.
- **Dividend** — A share of company profits paid to shareholders.
- **Dollar-cost averaging** — Investing fixed amounts on a regular schedule regardless of price.
- **ETF** — Exchange-traded fund; a basket of securities trading like a stock.
- **Expense ratio** — A fund's annual fee as a percentage of assets.
- **Equity** — Ownership; another word for stock.
- **Index fund** — A fund that passively tracks a market benchmark at low cost.
- **Inflation** — The general rise in prices that erodes money's purchasing power.
- **Liquidity** — How easily an asset can be converted to cash without loss.
- **Maturity** — The date a bond repays its principal.
- **NAV** — Net asset value; the per-share value of a fund.
- **P/E ratio** — Price divided by earnings per share; a valuation gauge.
- **Portfolio** — The full collection of your investments.
- **Principal** — The original amount invested or lent.
- **Rebalancing** — Restoring a portfolio to its target allocation.
- **REIT** — Real Estate Investment Trust; a tradable real-estate vehicle.
- **Risk tolerance** — How much volatility you can emotionally withstand.
- **Roth** — An account funded with after-tax money that grows and withdraws tax-free.
- **Securities** — Tradable financial instruments like stocks and bonds.
- **Stock** — A share of ownership in a company.
- **Target-date fund** — An all-in-one fund that grows more conservative over time.
- **Time horizon** — How long until you need the money.
- **Volatility** — The degree of price fluctuation; a common risk proxy.
- **Yield** — Income from an investment as a percentage of its price.
